Project Overview Locked

1.1 Concept

A creature-collecting survival RPG where players capture, raise, and evolve creatures in a world that reveals its systems progressively. The game rewards curiosity, relationship-building, and strategic depth over grinding. Every enemy is capturable. The evolution system branches and converges like Digimon — the same creature can become radically different things depending on how it is raised.

The world does not change as the player progresses — the player's understanding of it deepens. Systems that seem opaque early become legible through play and exploration.

1.2 Core Design Pillars

PillarExpression
Think, don't grindCorrect loadout and strategy beats overleveled wrong loadout. Inspired by Sonny. The player who understands the system wins.
Relationship as progressionBond with each creature is a genuine progression axis. A 100% bond creature is permanently different — not just stronger, but changed.
Discovery over instructionThe four type axes are revealed progressively through four regions. No type guide is handed to the player. Understanding is earned.
Every creature is a choiceSpecies, Temper, Origin, Signal, Force, and Celestial (if applicable) all shape what a creature is. No two builds are identical.
The web, not the ladderEvolution is a web of branching and converging paths. Devolution is always available. The goal is the right form, not the highest tier.

3.1 Evolution Web

[DECISION LOCKED] Digimon-style web with branching and convergence. Every enemy capturable. Roster: ~66 base identities → ~200–250 total forms at launch.

Evolution trigger taxonomy — three types

Every branch in the evolution web is triggered by one of three mechanisms. The trigger type is a property of the specific branch, not the creature. A single species may have one branch that is standard, another that is item-gated, and a fusion path that requires a specific partner.

TypeTriggerWhat happensOn devolution
1. Standard Level floor met + up to 3 attribute minimums + up to 2 conditionals (see Branch Requirement Framework below) Creature evolves into the new form. Most branches are this type. Requirements vary per branch — simple branches may have only a level floor; exotic branches add Aptitude gates and conditionals. Creature returns. Skills banked, Bond preserved, Aptitude grown. All branches reopen. Met conditionals stay met.
2. Item-gated Standard threshold met (level floor + attributes + conditionals) AND specific key item consumed on top Item is consumed. Creature evolves into a branch only accessible via that item. The item is the final key on top of all other requirements. Creature returns AND item is returned alongside it. All branches reopen. Met conditionals stay met. The item key is returned so the player can re-evolve this branch.
3. Fusion Two or three specific creatures at matching tiers, one designated as primary Ingredients consumed. Entirely new designed creature born with its own identity. Primary choice determines which creature is created. All ingredients returned. Primary carries everything learned. Secondary/tertiary return frozen at moment of fusion.

Branch requirement framework — design guideline v0.7.0

[DESIGN GUIDELINE — not a hard engine restriction] This framework defines the vocabulary for evolution branch requirements. It exists to keep branch requirements readable and intentional during species design. Exceeding the guideline limits is allowed when the design genuinely calls for it — but should prompt the question: are all of these requirements necessary, or are some redundant?

Every standard and item-gated evolution branch has a requirement profile composed of three layers: a level floor, stat thresholds, and optionally one or two conditionals. The stat thresholds are the real gate. Level is not a progression wall — it is the mechanism by which stats grow, and a small anti-exploit floor to prevent instant re-evolution after devolving.

The two-layer stat system

Every creature has two distinct stat layers that both count toward evolution thresholds and all other stat calculations:

LayerWhat it isHow it growsUI display
Base stats The creature's current form stats — the standard attribute values at this tier and level. Grows through leveling at the current tier. Shaped by species ceiling, Temper direction, personality modifier, and player assignment. Resets to the tier's starting values when devolving back to this form. Always shown. The primary stat display.
[Unnamed] pool A second persistent stat layer representing accumulated commitment to this creature's lineage across all devolution cycles. Not tied to any one form — it belongs to the creature's full history. Grows progressively with each devolution cycle. The more the player has cycled through this creature's evolution web, the larger this pool becomes. Cannot decrease. Persists through all forms including fusion. Shown separately alongside base stats. Naming pending — design before naming.
Design intent: The unnamed pool means two creatures at the same tier and level are not identical if one has been cycled through the evolution web more. A creature on its fifth devolution cycle carries a larger pool into the same T2 form as a freshly captured creature. It may hit evolution thresholds faster — not because it was grinded, but because it was lived in. The pool is a mechanical expression of the player's relationship with that creature's lineage.

Branch requirement layers

LayerGuidelineNotes
Level floor Always present. Low per-tier floor — just enough to prevent instant re-evolution, not a real wall. The level floor is not a shared gate like "all T2 branches require level 20." It is a lightweight anti-exploit: after devolving, a creature cannot immediately re-evolve because it needs to spend at least minimal time at this tier again. The floor is designed to be low enough that natural play hits it quickly. Exact values pending progression loop calibration. Different branches at the same tier do not need different level floors — the stat thresholds create the real differentiation between simple and exotic branches.
Stat thresholds Up to 3 stats as guideline. No hard cap — exceed when design requires it. Both stat layers (base stats + unnamed pool) count toward thresholds. Stat thresholds are the primary evolution gate. The primary route of any species is designed so that natural leveling at that tier — following the creature's Temper direction — reaches the easiest branch thresholds organically at the expected progression pace. Alternate and exotic branches require stats harder to reach naturally: either a stat the Temper doesn't push high, or an Aptitude level that requires prior devolution cycling. Choosing which stats to gate on is a design signal about what the form embodies.
Conditionals Up to 2 conditionals as guideline. No hard cap — exceed when design requires it. Conditionals are one-time checks, not ongoing requirements. Once met, they stay met across all devolution cycles — a player who defeated the required boss does not need to defeat it again on the next cycle. Conditionals communicate something about the branch's identity beyond stats: a boss kill says "this form knows that enemy." A captured creature says "this form has a relationship with that species."

Valid conditional types

Conditional typeExampleDesign intent
Boss defeated Must have defeated Ashenveil (Region 2 boss) at least once The creature has faced and overcome something specific. The branch represents a form forged by that encounter. Particularly appropriate for Corrupted or Primordial evolutions.
Item in possession Must have a Void Shard in inventory (item not consumed — just checked) Different from item-gated evolution where the item is spent. Here the item is a key held in inventory — the player must have found or crafted it, but keeps it. Signals a connection to where the item came from.
Creature captured or befriended Must have a Torrenth at 40%+ bond in current roster The creature's evolution is shaped by a relationship with another species. Creates natural narrative links between species lines. Especially powerful for convergence branches in the evolution web.

Region reached Must have reached Region 3 (Ancient World) World-progress gate. Appropriate for forms whose lore or typing is tied to a specific region. Ensures certain exotic evolutions are not accessible too early in the game.
Origin type Creature must currently have Corrupted or Hollow Origin The creature's philosophical nature gates this branch. A form that embodies corruption should require the creature to have walked that path. Creates meaningful interaction with the Origin system and re-tempering/origin-change mechanics.

Branch multiplicity and mutual exclusivity

RuleDetail
Branches per tier transition Minimum 2, maximum 5 branches from any given form to the next tier. Most transitions will have 2-3. 5 is reserved for T4→T5 Transcendent where the full complexity of the evolution web converges.
Mutual exclusivity Branches are mutually exclusive at the moment of evolution — taking one branch makes others unavailable while in that form. Devolving always reopens all branches. The full evolution web is explorable over time by cycling through forms.
Met conditions persist Once a conditional is met it stays met across all devolution cycles. Boss defeated, region reached, skill tree unlocked — all stay checked permanently. The level floor is the only requirement that resets on devolution, since it is an anti-exploit measure rather than a one-time achievement.
Aptitude as exotic gate High Aptitude as a stat threshold is the primary signal for exotic and rare evolution branches. Aptitude grows through evolution/devolution cycling. A high-Aptitude branch rewards players who have explored the evolution web deeply — a freshly captured creature at the same level cannot reach it. This is the intended design: exotic forms require lived investment, not just time spent grinding.
Unnamed pool as secondary path-opener Because the unnamed pool accumulates across all devolution cycles and counts toward stat thresholds, a creature on its third or fourth cycle through a tier may hit alternate branch thresholds faster than it did on its first pass. The level floor still applies — preventing instant re-evolution — but the stat work required is reduced. This creates a natural reward for players who engage deeply with the evolution cycling system.

Branch visibility — what the player sees

ElementVisible?Detail
Stat requirements Always shown The player can always see exactly what stats are needed for each available branch. Both layers (base stat + unnamed pool) are shown against the threshold. The player knows what they need to build toward.
Conditional requirements Always shown Conditionals are shown. The player can see "Boss defeated: Ashenveil" as a requirement and know what they need to do — even if they haven't done it yet.

3.2 Devolution — Finalized v0.7.0

[DECISION LOCKED] Devolution moves a creature back exactly one tier per action. A creature at T5 devolves to T4. To reach T1 from T5 requires four separate devolve actions. Multiple devolutions can be performed in a row, but each moves only one tier. This mirrors Digimon's approach and keeps the UI simple — the player always has exactly two directions: forward into evolution branches, or back one tier.

Core devolution rules

RuleDetail
One tier per actionT5 → T4 only. Cannot skip tiers. Cannot jump T5 → T2 in one action. Multiple devolves can be chained immediately — the player chooses when to stop.
Always availableNo condition, no time limit, no cost. Applies to all forms including item-gated branches and fusion forms.
Bond preservedBond percentage is fully preserved through all devolution. Bond floor at the last milestone reached is maintained.
All branches reopenDevolving one tier reopens all evolution branches at the previous tier. Branches not taken are available again. The full evolution web is explorable over time.
Met conditionals persistBoss defeated, region reached, skill tree unlocked, creature captured — all stay met permanently across all devolutions. They are one-time checks, never reset.
Level floor resetsThe anti-exploit level floor resets on devolution — the creature must spend minimal time at the tier before evolving again. This prevents instant re-evolution cycles. All other requirements (stat thresholds, conditionals) remain met.
Item-gated branchesDevolving from an item-gated form returns the consumed item alongside the creature. The player can re-evolve that branch by spending the item again.
Fusion formsDevolving from a fusion form returns all ingredient creatures. Primary returns with everything the fusion form learned. Secondary and tertiary return frozen as they were at the moment of fusion.

Skill access — full library, all tiers, no efficacy penalty

[DECISION LOCKED] All skills learned at any tier are accessible at all times, regardless of the creature's current tier. A creature at T1 that has previously learned T4 skills can equip and use those T4 skills. There is no efficacy penalty or tier distance modifier. The stat system provides the natural balancing — a T4 skill used at T1 fires at full mechanical effect, but Force, Resonance, Aura, and other stats are at T1 levels, so raw output is naturally lower. The skill library grows in one direction only: it never shrinks.
Skill stateDescriptionUsable?
LearnedSkills developed at any previously visited tier, including all higher tiers.Yes — fully equippable and usable at any tier with no penalty.
Not yet learnedSkills at tiers not yet reached, or skills at any tier not yet unlocked through use.No — not yet discovered.

How tier gap is expressed without a penalty modifier

The tradeoff of using a high-tier skill at a low tier is entirely expressed through the stat system. No separate modifier is needed:

Skill typeHow tier gap is felt
Damage skillsDeal damage scaled by Force or Resonance. At T1 these are low — output is naturally reduced compared to using the same skill at T5.
Aura / energy skillsGenerate or cost Aura scaled by the Aura attribute. Lower Aura stat at T1 means the energy economy is tighter.
Status application skillsApplication chance and duration scaled by Resonance or Resolve (enemy). Lower Resonance at T1 = lower application rate.
Defensive / buff skillsScale with Guard, Ward, or Vitality. Lower defensive stats at T1 mean defensive skills provide proportionally smaller absolute values.
Instinct-based skillsTiming windows, combo chance, and reaction triggers scale with Instinct. Lower Instinct at T1 means harder execution and lower proc rates.

Design implication — skill scaling guideline

[DESIGN GUIDELINE] Every skill must scale with at least one attribute. A skill with purely flat effects that ignore all stats entirely would be equally powerful at T1 and T5 — removing the natural tier gap that the stat system provides. This guideline applies during all skill design phases. Flat-seeming effects (e.g. "apply Burn") should have at least one stat-scaled element: duration scaled by Resonance, application chance scaled by Aura, or an additional stat-scaled component alongside the flat trigger.
AcceptableNot acceptable
"Apply Burn — duration scaled by Resonance""Apply Burn — always 3 turns regardless of stats"
"Recover 15% of max Aura — max Aura scales with Aura attribute""Recover exactly 30 Aura regardless of pool size"
"Create territory — radius and duration scaled by Force""Create territory — always covers 3 tiles for 2 turns"
"Reduce next attack — reduction amount scaled by Guard""Block the next attack completely regardless of Guard"

Note: percentage-based effects naturally inherit stat scaling because the underlying pool (max HP, max Aura) is stat-dependent. "Recover 15% max Aura" scales correctly because max Aura is driven by the Aura attribute. Watch for effects that reference fixed numeric values rather than percentages or stat multiples.

The strategic upside of full skill access: a player who has cycled through T5 and returns to T1 can equip a diverse loadout drawing from their entire history. A T1 creature with a T4 skill library is genuinely different from a freshly captured T1 — not just richer in options, but potentially comparable in raw power to a freshly evolved T5 if the unnamed pool is large enough.

Veteran T1 vs fresh T5 — intentional design

[INTENTIONAL DESIGN — do not balance away] A sufficiently veteran T1 creature can match or exceed the raw stats of a freshly evolved T5 through unnamed pool accumulation. This is correct and intended. Future designers should not treat this as a balance bug.
Veteran T1 (many high-tier cycles)Fresh T5 (first evolution)
Base statsLow — T1 ceilingHigh — T5 ceiling
Unnamed poolLarge — accumulated across many cycles through T5Tiny or zero — little cycling history
Combined statsPotentially comparable or exceeding fresh T5High base, minimal pool
Skill slots2 active slots (T1 limit)5-7 active slots (T5)
Skill libraryFull T1-T5 library accessibleOnly skills learned so far
Evolution burstT1 burst capabilityT5 burst capability — significantly stronger
What it representsEnormous investment of time, cycling, and bondRecent achievement — earned through normal progression

The veteran T1 is not overpowered — it is differently powerful. Its fewer active skill slots and lower evolution burst capability mean it cannot simply outperform a veteran T5 in all dimensions. But it represents a different shape of strength that rewards a specific style of engagement: deep cycling, high bond, and long commitment to a single creature lineage.

The player who has cycled a creature to T5 and back fifteen times has built something that cannot be rushed. The unnamed pool cannot be bought or skipped — it requires genuinely spending time at high tiers with high bond. A veteran T1 being able to compete with a fresh T5 is the system correctly recognising and rewarding that investment.

Unnamed pool — growth per tier-step

Each devolution step triggers unnamed pool growth. The growth amount is not fixed — it scales with how far past the tier's base stat floor the creature developed before devolving:

ScenarioPool gain per stepWhy
Devolve immediately after reaching tier minimumVery smallBarely exceeded the base stat floor. Little excess development to bank.
Devolve after moderate time at tierModerateMeaningful stat growth above floor. Proportional reward.
Devolve after deep development at tier (overleveled)LargeSignificant excess stats accumulated. Full reward for commitment.
Devolve from T5 after full developmentLargestHighest tier = highest floor = highest excess potential. Deepest investment rewarded most.

Anti-exploit design: A player who farms T1↔T2 repeatedly gains far less unnamed pool than one who pushes to T4 or T5 before devolving. Low tiers have low base stat floors — the creature hits them quickly and excess is minimal. High tiers have high floors — the creature must genuinely develop to exceed them. The reward structure makes deep-tier exploration naturally more efficient than low-tier farming. No hard rule is needed; the math disincentivises the exploit inherently.

Pool growth also triggers on evolution: Overleveling at a tier before evolving contributes pool growth when the evolution step is taken. A player who stays at T3 well past the evolution threshold arrives at T4 with a slightly larger pool from their extended T3 time — the system rewards patience and thoroughness at each form, not just cycling speed.

Bond multiplier: The unnamed pool growth per step is scaled by the creature's current Bond level. Higher bond = larger pool gain. Both the depth of stat development and the relationship investment compound together into the pool.

3.2b Item-Gated Evolution — Finalized v0.7.0

[DECISION LOCKED] A variant of standard evolution where a specific key item must be consumed alongside the normal evolution threshold. The item is consumed on evolution. On devolution, the item is returned alongside the creature — the player retains the ability to re-access this branch. This is not a separate system — it is a branch property within the existing evolution web.

Rules

RuleDetail
Still requires thresholdItem-gated evolution does not bypass standard evolution requirements. The creature must still meet the stat, Bond, or Aptitude threshold for that branch. The item is an additional requirement on top of — not instead of — normal evolution conditions.
Item is consumedThe key item is permanently consumed at the moment of evolution. It is not held, not equipped, not retained. It is spent to open the branch.
Devolution returns the itemIf the player devolves from an item-gated form, the item is returned alongside the creature in the same state as when it was spent. The player can re-evolve the branch by spending the item again. This is consistent with the devolution philosophy — going back does not destroy what you invested.
Branch exclusivitySome item-gated branches may be mutually exclusive with other branches at the same tier — choosing to spend the item closes off an alternative standard path. This is a per-branch design decision made during species roster design.
Item design spaceKey items are a rich design space: rare world drops, crafted from specific materials, boss rewards, drops from specific creature species, items tied to world lore. An item required to evolve a Corrupted Origin creature might only be obtainable from Corrupted creature encounters. The item and the evolution branch should feel thematically connected.

Example

CreatureStandard T4 branchItem-gated T4 branchItem source
Embrak (Fire, T3) Embrak → Cindrath (standard Fire T4) Embrak → Ashenmaw (Shadow/Fire T4, item required) Void Shard — rare drop from Shadow-type bosses in Region 2+

Ashenmaw's Shadow/Fire dual typing is only accessible through this branch. A player who devolves from Ashenmaw gets their Void Shard back and can re-evolve later. A player who never finds or crafts a Void Shard follows the standard Cindrath path instead. Both paths are valid — item-gating creates optional depth, not mandatory content.

Relationship to Fusion

Item-gated evolution and Fusion are different mechanics that serve different roles. Item-gated evolution keeps the creature on its own evolution web — the same creature, a different branch. Fusion consumes multiple creatures to birth something entirely new. The distinction is: item-gated is still this creature becoming something. Fusion is these creatures ending so something new can begin.

3.3 Fusion — Finalized v0.7.0

[DECISION LOCKED] Fusion is a permanent creation act — not a temporary battle mechanic. Two or three specific creatures are consumed to birth an entirely new designed creature. Reference model: Omnimon (Digimon), created from WarGreymon + MetalGarurumon. The fusion creature is a distinct entity with its own identity, typing, lore, and Bond-100 skill.

Core rules

RuleDetail
Fixed recipesEach fusion requires specific named creatures at specific tier thresholds. No substitutions. Flexible recipes (any two matching criteria) are flagged as future design exploration — requires solving sprite and typing generation first.
Ingredient countTwo or three creatures. Most fusions use two. Three-creature fusions are rarer and produce more powerful results. Determined per recipe during species roster design.
Tier requirementsEach recipe defines its own tier requirements, set during roster design. Design guideline (not hard rule): both/all ingredients should be at the same tier. This preserves the sense that fusion is two equals combining — not one creature carrying another. Tier-mismatched fusions require explicit justification in the recipe notes. Most fusions will be same-tier by default.
Primary designationPlayer designates one ingredient as Primary before fusing. Different primary = different fusion creature. Embrak (primary) + Torrenth (secondary) produces a different creature than Torrenth (primary) + Embrak (secondary). Both are fully designed entities.
Bond inheritancePrimary's bond carries through continuously. The fusion form continues the primary's bond arc — not a new relationship. The primary's bond floor (last milestone reached) is preserved.
DevolutionAlways available, same rules as normal devolution. No condition, no time limit.
What returns on devolveAll ingredient creatures are returned. Primary returns with everything the fusion form learned — skills banked, Bond preserved and grown, Aptitude increased, attributes banked at current bond rate. Secondary and tertiary return frozen exactly as they were at the moment of fusion. Nothing learned.
Celestial tierSome fusion creatures are T6/T7 Celestial tier. The rarest and most powerful fusions reach Celestial. Celestial property and lore are designed together alongside the fusion creature, following the same principle as evolution-path Celestial creatures.

Fusion flow

StepWhat happens
1. Meet recipeAll ingredient creatures must be at their required tiers and be the specific species named in the recipe.
2. Choose primaryPlayer designates which ingredient is primary. This determines which of the two possible fusion creatures is created and whose bond arc continues.
3. Fusion occursIngredients are consumed. New fusion creature is created with its own Primal typing, Signal pool, Tempo type, Origin, and lore. Inherits primary's bond percentage.
4. Play the fusionFusion creature is played normally — levels, bonds, banks attributes and skills. Its experience all counts toward the primary's progression arc.
5. Devolve (any time)All ingredients returned. Primary carries everything learned. Secondary/tertiary return frozen at fusion state. Player can re-fuse at any time by meeting requirements again.

Future design exploration

Flexible fusion recipes — where any two creatures meeting defined criteria (matching Origin, complementary Primal types, etc.) can fuse into a procedurally determined result — are flagged as a future design consideration. Requires solving: sprite generation for all possible combinations, typing rules for any valid pair, and balance implications. Not in scope for current design phases.

Item + creature fusion — a variant where a creature fuses with a key item rather than another creature to produce a new fusion entity. This is currently handled as item-gated evolution (§3.2b). If a future case arises where the result feels more like a new creature than an evolution branch, this may be revisited as a fusion variant. The distinction test: does the result feel like this creature transformed (evolution) or something new born from this creature and this object (fusion)?

Interference: Adds noise — enemy next skill may have reduced effect or elevated cost.

Interference: Status effects on enemies generate Aura for you — enemy suffering feeds your engine.
Current

Interference: Forces enemy Aura to flow — must spend it this turn or lose it.

[DECISION LOCKED] Status effects are the engine of the combat loop — apply status → create vulnerability → exploit it. Signal typing drives application and disruption. Progression Loop Design Locked v0.7.9

21.1 Overview

[DECISION LOCKED] The progression loop is built around emergent reward — rewards come from exploration and discovery rather than scheduled drops. The game respects variable session lengths: a 20-minute session yields meaningful bond and stat progress; a 3-hour session can push through an evolution cycle, defeat a mini-boss, and unlock a new sub-region. Neither depth nor breadth of roster development is penalised.

21.2 Core Loop Structure

LayerCycle lengthWhat happens
Battle loopMinutesEncounter enemies, apply type knowledge, use status chains, build bond. Win → stat growth + XP + drops. Lose → resource loss only, no progress lost. Retry with knowledge of what went wrong.
Exploration loop15-30 minMove through sub-regions, discover hidden areas, encounter new creatures, find lore fragments. Emergent rewards — what you find depends on where you go and what creatures you bring.
Development loop1-3 hoursAccumulate enough stat growth to hit an evolution threshold or bond milestone. Evolving, devolving, and re-evolving generates unnamed pool growth. Bond milestones unlock skill trees.
Regional loopMulti-sessionExplore region, defeat 5+ mini-bosses in any order, collect quest items, craft access key, challenge and defeat regional boss. Boss defeat opens next region or deeper current-region access.
World loopFull playthroughFour main regions, each revealing a new type axis. Story gates between regions. Expandable via additional regions post-launch.

21.3 Stat and Level Growth

Battles (won)Primary XP source → level → base stats. Small bond gain.The main engine of stat growth. Winning battles at a tier accumulates XP toward leveling, which grows base stats according to species ceiling and Temper direction.

Fast early, progressively slower at each tier. T1→T2 should feel quick — the player is learning the system and early payoffs build investment. Each subsequent tier requires meaningfully more time. RuleDetail
No progress lossLosing a battle never reverses XP, stat growth, bond, or evolution state. The player always keeps what they built.

21.8 Roster Development Philosophy

The player chooses depth vs breadth freely. Both approaches are valid and neither is punished:

ApproachWhat the game rewards
Deep — one creature developed fullyLarge unnamed pool, veteran T1 viability, deep skill library, high bond unlocks, full evolution web explored. The creature becomes a singular identity.
Broad — many creatures developed in parallelType coverage for creature-specific sub-regions, access to more fusion combinations, flexibility in team composition for different regional challenges.
HybridMost players will naturally land here — one or two primary creatures with a supporting cast. The systems reward both dimensions simultaneously.

Creature-specific sub-regions (requiring specific creature types or bond levels) provide a natural incentive for breadth without mandating it. A player who never diversifies can still complete all main content — sub-regions are optional depth, not required gates.
